The Sentiment Polarity Category classifies how positively or negatively people talk of tweets about rivers on Twitter. Each tweet is assigned a score, which can have a between -5 (extremely negative) and 5 (extremely positive) but will usually fall somewhere in between.

Tweets are aggreagtaed at each geographical scale (River Basin District, Management Catchment, Operational Catchment and Waterbody) and classified into three categories; tweets with a polarity score in the lower third ("Negative Sentiment Engagement") is a score between -3.336 to -.0.59 and are coloured red; tweets with a polarity score in the middle third ("Neutral Sentiment Engagement") is a score between -0.059 and 0.303 and are coloured yellow; tweets with a polarity score in the upper third ("Positive Sentiment Engagement") is a score between 0.303 and 7.857 and are coloured green. The Sentiment Polarity Category tells us how people feel when they mention a river in their tweets. Sentiment polarity means whether the feelings they express in the language of their tweets are positive or negative (or neutral).

How does the Sentiment Polarity Category algorithm work?

First, we assign each tweet a score between 10 for extremely positive and -10 for extremely negative (but tweets will usually fall somewhere in between). Then, we aggregate tweets at four geographical scales, namely river basin district, management catchment, operational catchment, and water body. At each scale, we then sort tweets classified into three categories: Negative Sentiment Engagement, which comprises tweets with a polarity score in the lower third (between -3.336 and -0.59) and is coloured red on the map; Neutral Sentiment Engagement, which comprises tweets with a polarity score in the middle third (between - 0.059 and 0.303) and is coloured yellow; and Positive Sentiment Engagement, which comprises tweets with a polarity score in the upper third (between 0.303 and 7.857) and is coloured green. Each category contains roughly the same number of tweets.
